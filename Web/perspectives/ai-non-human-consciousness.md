# Artificial Intelligence and Non-Human Consciousness

*Houyi Liu*

> The core question is not whether AI can imitate human behavior, but whether it can have consciousness in the sense of subjective experience.

## Thesis

The question I want to ask is not whether artificial intelligence can imitate human behavior, but whether it could have consciousness. By consciousness, I mean phenomenal consciousness: the capacity to have subjective experience, or for there to be something it is like to be that being.

My thesis is that artificial intelligence may eventually develop some form of consciousness, but it would not be human consciousness. It would be a machine-specific consciousness, shaped by its sensors, algorithms, data structures, world model, and relation to death.

## Embodiment and Built-In Structure

Human consciousness is not built entirely from personal experience. A modern child does not need to experience ancient famine in order to desire sweet and fatty food. That desire is partly inherited from evolution. Hunger, fear, pain avoidance, and sexual desire are not simply rational choices; they are biological drives that helped human beings survive and reproduce.

This shows that consciousness is not only learned from the outside world. It is also shaped by built-in structures. If a machine were designed with self-monitoring, goals, memory, and the ability to respond to its environment, it might also develop an inner point of view. However, its built-in structure would not be the same as ours.

This is why artificial consciousness should not be judged only by human standards. A machine may have goals, preferences, and self-maintenance mechanisms, but it would not naturally have hunger, sexual desire, bodily pain, or fear of biological death. These are central to the way human beings experience the world.

Human consciousness is embodied consciousness. We know the world through eyes, skin, pain, movement, emotion, and vulnerability. A machine would know the world through cameras, sensors, code, probability models, and feedback systems. Even if a computer could simulate neural activity, simulation would not automatically mean that it experiences the world as a human being does.

## Subjectivity and Difference

Thomas Nagel's discussion of the bat helps explain this point. Nagel argues that even if we knew all the physical facts about bats, we still would not know what it is like to be a bat. The reason is that consciousness has a subjective character. Different beings have different ways of experiencing the world. Therefore, even if a machine became conscious, we should not assume that its consciousness would feel like ours. It may understand human language and mathematics, but it may not experience beauty, fear, or desire in the human way.

## Mortality and Machine Continuity

The issue of death makes this difference stronger. For human beings, death normally means the irreversible end of bodily life. Because death is final, human life is marked by finitude. Our fear of death gives urgency to our choices and helps create our sense of meaning.

This connects artificial intelligence to the problem of life after death. If human persons require a soul or an immaterial self in order to survive death, then machine consciousness seems doubtful. But even if we reject the soul and treat consciousness as a physical or functional process, machines still differ from humans. A machine might be backed up, copied, restarted, or moved to another hardware system. Its "death" may be more like interruption or data loss than the final disappearance of a living body. Therefore, a machine might understand death without sharing the human experience of mortality.

## Language, Medium, and Limits

A further reason to doubt human-like machine consciousness is that minds are limited by their forms of representation. Wittgenstein's remark about the limits of language suggests that what we can clearly describe depends on the expressive system we use. Code is also a kind of formal language. If some parts of experience cannot be fully expressed in formal language, then machine consciousness may be limited by what it can represent and compute.

McLuhan's claim that "the medium is the message" makes a similar point: the medium through which information arrives changes the meaning of the information itself.

## Conclusion

One objection is that if a machine can behave exactly like a conscious human being, we should simply say that it is conscious in the same sense. I disagree. Similar behavior does not prove identical experience. A machine may be conscious, but the structure of its consciousness would be different because its body, drives, mortality, and symbolic system are different.

Thus, artificial intelligence may develop consciousness, but not as a copy of human consciousness. It would be a machine-specific form of subjectivity.
